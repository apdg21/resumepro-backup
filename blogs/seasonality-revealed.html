Seasonality Revealed: Evidence Beyond the Guesswork

Posted on July 7, 2026 | 7 min read
Featured image: 18-month email performance trend showing a spring slowdown followed by a July–October rebound

In our last post, we explained why guessing brands never discover seasonality. Their constant changes bury the signals that matter. But what happens when a brand operates with discipline? The answer is simple: seasonality emerges — clear, predictable, and powerful.

📊 The Data Speaks

From January 2025 through July 2026, the numbers tell a story:

  • A strong start, though the peak month shifts: January through March is generally solid for growth and revenue in both years, but the standout month moves around within the quarter — February 2025 was the real spike, while January 2026 led its own year. Q1 is dependable overall, just not down to the exact month.
  • Spring softens: April through June consistently underperforms the surrounding months. In 2025, this was a natural seasonal decline. In 2026, the same window was made deeper by a deliberate list hygiene suppression — inactive subscribers were removed in May to sharpen deliverability, on top of the seasonal dip already underway.
  • Late-summer strength: July through October consistently outperforms the spring months, with September 2025 standing out as the strongest month in the dataset (+3,538 net growth, €84.6k revenue).
  • Year-end cooldown: November and December ease off from the October high. The "Revival" label in the table reflects the analytics model's read on list health and efficiency for that month, not a jump in revenue — December 2025 revenue is in fact lower than October and November.

One year of this pattern could be luck. Seeing the same turning points repeat in a second year — spring softening, then a July–October rebound — is what starts to look like a genuine seasonal rhythm rather than noise. The data reveals a repeatable pattern, and it only becomes visible when a program is run with enough consistency to let it surface.

Notice the overall rhythm rather than any single month. Q1 opens strong, spring softens, and late summer accelerates through September and October before demand cools toward year-end — the same shape repeats across both years, even after the engaged list was deliberately trimmed in May 2026.

🔄 The Role of List Hygiene

The May 2026 decline wasn't a failure — it was a strategic choice. By suppressing unengaged subscribers, the brand shrank the list on paper while likely improving deliverability and sender reputation going forward.

To be fair to the data: the purge and the seasonal dip landed in the same month, so May 2026 on its own can't cleanly separate "list hygiene effect" from "seasonal effect" — the two are mixed together. What supports the seasonal read is that May 2025 shows the same relative softening without any purge at all. Seeing the dip in a year with no confound, and again in a year with one, is what makes the seasonal explanation more credible than coincidence.

Guessing brands would never get far enough to notice this. Their reactive changes would bury the signal before a comparison like this was even possible. Disciplined brands can prune, measure, and still track seasonality underneath the noise.

🧹 Pro Tip

Unsub rate didn't fall in a straight line after the May 2026 cleanup — it dipped to 1.97% in May, ticked back up to 2.42% in June, then read 0.52% in July, which is only a 7-day partial figure and not comparable to a full month. The honest read: post-cleanup unsub rate settled into a lower, tighter band (roughly 2.0–2.8%) than the pre-cleanup norm — a real improvement, just not a clean straight-line drop to under 1%.

✅ Lessons for Marketers

  • Consistency reveals rhythm: Keep content, cadence, and segmentation steady enough for patterns to emerge.
  • Year-over-year comparison: Don't just compare this month to last. Compare this May to last May. That's how seasonality shows itself.
  • List hygiene strengthens signals: Suppressing inactive subscribers makes your data cleaner and your seasonal patterns sharper.
  • Plan around cycles: Expect softer performance through spring, and stronger demand in Q1 and again from late summer through early autumn. Build promotions and content calendars accordingly.

🌟 Closing Thought

One year can be luck. Two years showing the same turning points is the beginning of a seasonal pattern. No dataset is perfect, and eighteen months is still an early read — but the repeated transition from a softer spring into a stronger July–October period gives marketers a far more reliable planning signal than month-to-month comparisons alone.

Seasonality isn't hidden from disciplined brands. It's revealed, month after month, year after year, in the rhythm of their audience. The evidence is in the data — and the confidence comes from knowing you're making decisions based on patterns, not panic.
